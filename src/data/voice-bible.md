# GOVERNED: Tone of Voice & Content Design Bible

Welcome, content designer. This guide establishes the writing style, narrative rules, and formatting standards for the modular cities of **GOVERNED**—a satirical, high-stakes municipal management simulation.

Use this document to draft new cities, advisors, decisions, and public feed updates that mesh perfectly with the game’s core mechanics and cynical comedic tone.

---

## 1. Core Narrative Pillars

### A. Bureaucratic Absurdity
The systems are too big, too old, and too corrupt to fix cleanly. Every solution creates a new, bizarre problem. Avoid "happily ever after" results; even the most "successful" policies should have side effects that make the local media sigh or mock you.

### B. High-Stakes Cynicism
You are managing real cities facing existential threats (sinking land, traffic gridlock, war, economic collapse). While the tone is comedic, the danger should feel real. The humour is dark, dry, and resigned.

### C. Hyper-Local Authenticity
Every city must sound like itself, not a generic template. Content must use real landmarks, local slang, specific industries, and real-world municipal frustrations. 
*   **Jakarta** talks about KRL rush hours, Monas, ojek drivers, and says *"Ya Allah, macet lagi."*
*   **San Francisco** talks about Tenderloin, NIMBYs, tech IPOs, and says *"This is why I pay the most rent in America."*

## 2. Advisor Design Rules

Advisors are not objective experts; they are players in the municipal game with their own egos, agendas, and thresholds for betrayal.

### A. The Five Domains & Archetypes
Every city has a set of advisors covering key sectors, mapped to specific political/narrative archetypes:
1.  **Finance & Budget** (*The Pragmatist*): Focused purely on the bottom line. Speaks in cold numbers, balance sheets, and threats from credit rating agencies.
2.  **Security & Order** (*The Hardliner*): Obsessed with control, law enforcement, and stability. Always advocates for direct intervention.
3.  **Urban Planning & Environment** (*The Idealist*): Driven by long-term planning, sustainability, and data. Often ignored and prone to high-minded sarcasm.
4.  **Transport & Infrastructure** (*The Fixer*): Focuses on roads, transit lines, and construction. Often has hidden ties to local developers.
5.  **Community & Religious Affairs** (*The Moralist*): The voice of the grassroots. Focuses on faith, social issues, and community trust.

### B. Dialogue Templates & Style
*   **Briefing**: Needs to state the current problem with a cynical or dry spin.
*   **Agreement**: Relieved, slightly smug, or validating.
*   **Disagreement**: Critical, passive-aggressive, or flatly warning you of future blame.
*   **Betrayal**: The point of no return. The advisor breaks from your administration and works against you, citing their hidden agenda trigger.
*   **Roast**: High-potency insults about the governor’s decisions.
*   **Scandal Reaction**: Plausible deniability, panic, or self-preservation.

## 3. Decision & Option Anatomy

Every turn, the governor is faced with a critical municipal decision. Options must fall into three distinct philosophical tags:

### Tag Categories:
*   **`SAFE` (Incremental / Bureaucratic)**:
    *   *The Strategy*: Mild, standard government solution.
    *   *The Trade-off*: High cost (budget), moderate/positive approval, low scandal risk.
*   **`BOLD` (Aggressive / High-Risk)**:
    *   *The Strategy*: Direct action or structural shift that disrupts the status quo.
    *   *The Trade-off*: Moderate cost, high risk of alienating a specific advisor, moderate scandal risk.
*   **`CHAOS` (Unconventional / Absurdist)**:
    *   *The Strategy*: A bizarre, PR-heavy, or deeply corrupt maneuver.
    *   *The Trade-off*: Very low cost (sometimes generates cash), high approval/disapproval volatility, and very high scandal risk.

## 4. Public Feed (Comment Library) Guidelines

The public feed simulates a mock social media scrolling feed that reflects the city's temperature. Make comments short, punchy, and modern.

*   **`positive`**: Reluctant praise. ("I'm in shock, they actually fixed the street.")
*   **`neutral`**: Apathy and fatigue. ("Another press release, same traffic.")
*   **`negative`**: Snarky complaints. ("Where is the budget going? My scooter drowned.")
*   **`crisis`**: Real-time panic and dark humor. ("I am currently in a kayak. Good job government.")
*   **`scandal`**: Outrage at corruption. ("Surprised? No. Disappointed? Always.")
